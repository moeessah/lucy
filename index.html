Test and learn lab

Test design should be as clear as the dashboard.

Two rules before any test launches: OOS confirmed clear in the test stores, and the go/no-go decision written down before the data comes back. Skipping either one makes the readout noise.

Test 01

Breakers counter display in Casey's and Pilot doors

The capsule mechanic is something a cashier can demonstrate in the transaction window. Put Breakers on the counter in top-velocity fuel and truck stop doors and read trial versus matched controls with standard shelf placement only.

Design400 test doors, 400 matched controls across Casey's and Pilot clusters
ReadoutUnits per reliable active door per week, 4-week hold
DecisionScale if incremental velocity clears +0.8 ppd and ROI exceeds 2.0x
RiskOOS during test window masks real demand. Confirm before launch, not after.
Test 02

Mint 8mg and Mango 4mg replenishment rescue

Some stores that look weak are really stockout stores. A case-pack buffer in the top 250 high-OOS doors can reveal demand the regular velocity report is actively hiding.

DesignTop 250 doors by OOS frequency, matched to 250 controls
ReadoutLost-sales recovery and sustained velocity improvement
DecisionOperationalize buffer if OOS rate falls below 5% and holds
RiskOverstock accumulates in low-velocity doors if segmentation is not tight
Test 03

Strength ladder assortment by channel

A fuel-stop shopper buying Breakers for the first time and a drug store customer planning nicotine reduction have different trip missions, different strength needs, and different explanations at the shelf. Test whether strength mix by channel drives incremental velocity before assuming one assortment works everywhere.

DesignThree assortment cells: c-store, drug, grocery
ReadoutDoor-weighted velocity and OOS rate per cell
DecisionCommit channel-specific planogram if velocity lifts above 10%
RiskToo many SKUs fragments turns and confuses replenishment
Test 04

Gum availability and confirmed-stocked readout

The current Gum velocity read is distorted because the denominator includes locator-listed stores that never stocked it. Build a confirmed-stocked universe first, then test whether a clearer shelf message lifts conversion.

Design150 confirmed Gum-stocked doors from scan data, not locator
ReadoutConversion per confirmed-stocked door, repeat rate
DecisionExpand only if repeat purchase holds at two-plus cycles
RiskLocator denominator inflates the baseline and makes a good result look flat
Test 05

Cashier explanation card at register

One crisp sentence about the Breakers capsule, placed at the register in doors where LUCY has shelf presence but conversion trails benchmark. If the ten-second explanation moves units, it scales at near-zero cost.

DesignHigh-frequency c-store and fuel-stop clusters, 200 doors
ReadoutTrial lift vs matched stores without card, sustained over four weeks
DecisionScale if repeat rate holds after the prompt. Drop if it only drives one-time trial.
RiskTraining compliance fades if the card relies on staff explanation, not passive placement
Test 06

Regional flavor-led secondary placement

Flavor earns attention on the floor faster than a full-assortment wall earns anything. Feature the top three regional flavor winners in secondary placement and read whether net category lift is real or just cannibalization of the main shelf.

DesignTop 3 regional flavors by velocity, not national default
ReadoutIncremental units vs main shelf cannibalization
DecisionScale only where net category lift is confirmed. Kill if it is just shuffling the same purchase.
RiskState flavor restrictions and retailer-specific policy on secondary placement

Scale

  • Matched-store incremental velocity clears the target set before launch.
  • Gross margin after trade spend stays attractive enough to justify the rollout.
  • Store execution is reproducible without founder-level attention or field heroics.

Iterate

  • Demand signal is positive but creative, placement, or stock execution is pulling it down.
  • Lift concentrates in a specific store format, daypart, or product that the original design did not isolate.
  • Field notes explain why the first run was noisy and the next one should be cleaner.

Kill

  • Clean execution, confirmed clean. No incrementality in the read. Move resources to the next test.
  • Promo takes from existing LUCY SKUs without expanding category sales at all.
  • Complexity is too high for the sales and field team to sustain without it degrading in month two.
Store-level execution checklist

A dashboard works when it sends someone to the right shelf with the right question.

Every store visit should answer all four of these before reporting back. Skipping one is how bad execution gets misread as bad demand.

A
AvailabilityIs the authorized SKU physically present, scanning correctly, priced, and on a replenishment path that keeps up with the store's velocity?
B
VisibilityIs LUCY findable on the adult nicotine path or checkout area without a staff explanation? Would a first-time shopper notice it?
C
MessageDoes the store explain the Breakers capsule in one glance? Does anything communicate what distinguishes LUCY from the shelf beside it?
D
DecisionWhat specific action can the rep, account lead, or ops team take this week based on what the visit found? If there is no action, the visit did not finish.

90-day build sequence

From retailer data cleanup to independent ownership.

Days 1–30

  • Map the door universe from locator, scan data, order history, and any existing field audit records. Reconcile what locator says against what is actually stocked.
  • Validate the Great Lakes and Mid-Atlantic cluster counts specifically. Those two regions represent roughly 59% of the listed footprint. If the active-store reality there is weaker than it looks, the national model needs to shift before any budget conversations happen.
  • Shadow at least three store visits before reading any internal analytics. The shelf tells you things the spreadsheet never will.
  • Build a clean weekly velocity view with every caveat called out plainly. No hidden denominators.

Days 31–60

  • Launch the Breakers counter display test in a matched set of Casey's or Pilot doors. OOS confirmed clear before launch date. Decision criteria written down before the data arrives.
  • Launch the Mint 8mg and Mango 4mg replenishment rescue in the top 250 high-OOS doors. Read whether the velocity recovery is real or temporary.
  • Build a store execution score the sales team can use in their weekly calls without asking analytics for a translation.
  • Every readout separates consumer demand signal from retailer operations noise. Decision and cause are always visible in the same view.

Days 61–180

  • Scale the winning test by door cluster type, not by chain average. Casey's c-store economics and drug channel economics are different and deserve different rollout pacing.
  • Own the Breakers counter display rollout fully: SKU authorization, field execution brief, weekly velocity readout, retailer account communication.

Market read

Category signals that shape the retail model.

The public data points to one operating truth: retail availability, adult-only compliance, product explanation, and fast test discipline matter as much as the media plan. Probably more.

Product architecture

  • Breakers: pouch with a crushable flavor and hydration capsule. Available in 4mg and 8mg. The capsule mechanic is the differentiation story at the shelf level.
  • Pouches: 4mg, 8mg, and 12mg across multiple flavors including Mint, Mango, Wintergreen, Espresso, and Cinnamon. Positioned for discreet, long-duration use.
  • Gum: 2mg, 4mg, and 6mg. Smaller retail footprint by design. Most listed locator stores do not carry it according to LUCY's own store locator. Treat it separately in every analytics readout.

Retail is where the category is won

  • Grand View Research estimates the U.S. nicotine pouch market at approximately $5.4B in 2025.
  • Offline retail accounted for roughly 96% of U.S. nicotine pouch revenue in 2025.
  • At 96% offline, retail velocity, shelf availability, and store execution are the growth levers. The brand with better shelf operations compounds faster than the brand with the better media plan.

Compliance layer

  • The FDA has authorized specific ZYN and selected nicotine pouch products. Not every pouch SKU in the category is authorized. LUCY operates within this framework carefully.
  • CDC and FDA guidance supports adult-only framing and prohibits cessation-aid claims. This shapes every piece of in-store communication.
  • Shipping restrictions and flavor rules vary by state and belong in the market eligibility model used for test design. Building a test in a restricted state wastes resources and can create compliance exposure.

Competitive pressure

  • ZYN sets the category benchmark for shelf presence and consumer awareness. Philip Morris International's distribution scale is the hardest thing to compete with directly.
  • on!, VELO, Rogue, and other pouch brands raise the bar on assortment discipline. A crowded shelf rewards the brand with the clearest explanation and punishes everyone else.
  • LUCY's Breakers format carries a trial story in-store that no big-tobacco-owned brand can match credibly. The activation needs to live on the shelf, not just in DTC creative.

Customer signal

  • Public Trustpilot themes show genuine product love on flavor and experience alongside complaints clustering around subscription friction, fulfillment delays, and occasional quality variance.
  • The pattern maps directly to which operational areas are leaking affinity the product earns. Repeat purchase rates, replacement orders, and product complaint flags belong in the same analytics view as velocity.
  • Subscription friction and fulfillment gaps show up in repeat purchase data before they show up in reviews. Monitoring both surfaces the problem earlier.

Store locator reality

  • 8,976 visible listed locations from the public locator screenshot taken May 6, 2026.
  • 5,400 to 7,200 is a modeled active-store range, not a public LUCY disclosure. It assumes 60 to 80 percent of listed doors are reliably stocked.
  • Reliable active doors are the denominator for velocity. Using the raw locator count inflates the base and makes every performance metric look worse than it is.
